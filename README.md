1 - Problem Statement
Electricians spend a lot of time on administrative tasks on top of the electrical work. Incoming job requests come from phone calls, emails, text messages, and more, and these can get messy and hard to organize. This leads to further issues with scheduling and managing urgency between jobs. As a result, the time they could spend with their families, or use to make more money on other electrical work, is wasted on busy work.
Another issue they deal with is buying materials they do not have in stock while at a job site. It can be hard to find a trustworthy store when far from their base location, which can lead to overpaying for materials and wasting time driving between several stores.
Small electrician businesses are especially affected by problems like these. There are currently several resources that can help with these problems, but there is no all-in-one platform, which forces electricians to use several separate apps that may not fully meet their needs.

2 - Selected Challenge Theme: Wildcard Challenge
Our project focuses on turning AI into a co-worker that can help electricians plan their days, organize their schedules, create material shopping lists, and become more efficient workers overall. We focus on how AI can support skilled trades rather than replace them. This directly aligns with the Wildcard Challenge, which is the theme we chose for this project.

3 - Solution Description
Our platform is an AI-powered management assistant designed specifically for electricians. It combines job management, intelligent scheduling, material planning, and nearby-job awareness for efficient routing into a single platform. By automating repetitive administrative tasks, electricians can spend more time completing jobs and less time organizing them.
The platform focuses on residential electrical repairs and fixes. When a request is instead a new installation or larger project, the platform recognizes it and flags it as outside repair scope, still capturing its urgency and location but not estimating materials, tools, or labor for it. 

Platform overview:
The platform is organized into four main areas the electrician moves between:
Requests: the home board where every incoming job appears as a card, automatically sorted by urgency. Each card summarizes the job and opens its full job page.
Scheduled: the jobs that have been booked, grouped by day with their confirmed time windows.
Purchase List: an aggregated materials shopping list for upcoming scheduled jobs, with optional AI price and store recommendations. 
Settings: the electrician's business details (name and base location), working hours, daily work cap, lunch break, target scheduling windows per urgency level, and the connected AI provider.
AI assists across these areas at three points: understanding incoming requests, recommending when to schedule each job, and building material shopping recommendations. The platform works with or without a connected AI provider (details in the AI approach and architecture section).

Workflow:
A written service request comes in from a client (phone call, email, text message, web form) and is brought into the platform through a .csv upload, with a direct connection to the electrician's website request form planned for the future.
The platform analyzes the request. This is where AI first comes in: client requests can be vague or complex, so AI interprets them so the rest of the system has a clear, structured understanding of each job. As part of this, the platform automatically flags safety hazards (for example, a burning smell, sparking, shock, or a total loss of power) as high urgency so they are prioritized, and distinguishes repair jobs from new installations or projects (which it marks as outside repair scope).
A job page is created from the request, and the job appears on the Requests board as a job card. The job page contains: the job's urgency, type, and part of the house; the estimated labor time; the client's details (name, address, contact information); materials (ranked by how likely each is to be needed) and the tools required; the key questions and uncertainties to resolve about the job, both before the visit and once on-site; and scheduling insights such as other nearby jobs, distance and ETA from the electrician's base location, and a recommended time window to propose to the client (based on the location of already-scheduled jobs and the job's urgency).
The electrician schedules the job. This can be done one job at a time from its job page, accepting the AI-recommended window or choosing a different one, or all at once with Auto-schedule from the Requests board, which books every unscheduled job to minimize total driving time while still prioritizing the most urgent jobs. If a job cannot be booked within the ideal window for its urgency (for example, an urgent job that cannot fit until later than its target), the job page flags this and prompts the electrician to confirm the urgency level and check that the client is okay with the next available opening. Once scheduled, the job card moves from Requests to Scheduled.
As soon as a job is scheduled, the platform opens the electrician's default email app with a personalized confirmation email drafted and ready to send to the client. If the client declines the proposed time, the job returns to the Requests board (flagged with a note to contact the client) so it can be re-scheduled.
On the Scheduled board, jobs are grouped by day, and each day includes a "See map" link that opens a Google Maps route through that day's jobs for efficient navigation.
Finally, the electrician can export the list of scheduled jobs as calendar events to their desired calendar app (Google Calendar, Apple Calendar, etc.).

Purchase list feature:
The Purchase List helps the electrician prepare and buy materials efficiently, directly addressing the time and money lost sourcing parts away from their base location.
For a chosen date range (defaulting to the next several work days), the platform gathers every material needed across the jobs scheduled in that range into a single shopping list. Materials that appear on more than one job are combined, and each item shows how many jobs need it and how likely it is to be needed. The electrician can adjust quantities and units and check off anything already in stock, which separates the list into "to buy" and "already in stock."
With an AI provider connected, the electrician can generate price and store recommendations for the "to buy" list. They first set a few preferences: the maximum number of stores they are willing to visit, the purchase method (pickup, delivery, and so on), and a location (defaulting to their base). The AI then returns one or more nearby store options, each with a short insight explaining why that store stands out (for example, cheapest overall or closest), an estimated distance and an estimated total for the full cart, per-item estimated prices with a link to each product, and a note for any item that store does not carry.
The electrician can re-sort the store options by cheapest cart or closest distance. When the connected AI provider supports live web search, prices and links are drawn from current store listings and the sources are listed; otherwise they are clearly labeled as estimates. The time the recommendations were last generated is shown, and they can be regenerated at any time. In short, this turns "figure out what to buy and where" into a single, ranked, comparison-ready list, helping the electrician avoid overpaying and avoid driving between multiple stores.

4 - AI Approach and Architecture
The platform uses a two-layer approach to AI, so it is useful on its own and far more capable with a connected AI provider.
The first layer is a built-in inference engine that runs entirely in the browser with no internet connection or API key. It reads each incoming request and classifies it (job type, urgency, scope, part of the house, likely materials, tools, and the questions to confirm). Because it is rule-based, it is fast and always available, but it is less confident on requests that are vague, complex, or full of typos. The two layers work together simply: the offline engine classifies every request instantly, and when an AI provider is connected its reading replaces the offline result, which remains the instant first pass and the fallback if an AI call fails.
The second layer is an optional AI provider. The electrician connects one by pasting an API key on the Settings page, and the platform is provider-agnostic: it is compatible with multiple AI providers, including IBM watsonx.ai, as well as Anthropic (Claude), OpenAI, Google (Gemini), xAI (Grok), Groq, Azure OpenAI, and any custom OpenAI-compatible endpoint. When a provider is connected, the AI re-reads every request for a higher-confidence understanding of the job and unlocks the price and store recommendations in the Purchase List.

Key points of the approach:
An API key is recommended for optimal functioning of the platform. Without one, the platform still works using the offline engine, but at a lower confidence level and with fewer features (for example, the "Recommendations & insights" price and store suggestions are unavailable).
An AI provider that supports live web search enables the most accurate "Recommendations & insights" feature in the Purchase List. Prices and store links are pulled from current online listings, and the sources are shown so they can be verified.
An AI provider that does not support live web search still powers "Recommendations & insights," but the prices it returns are estimates. These estimates cannot be verified against the store's website, and the platform labels them as estimates.
Some AI providers cannot be called directly from a browser (a security restriction called CORS). The platform routes those providers through a built-in secure relay automatically, so the electrician does not need to set anything up. Providers that allow direct browser access do not use the relay.

Each electrician signs in with an email and password, and their data (requests, schedule, settings, and API key) is stored in their own account in the platform's secure cloud database. Row-level security ensures each account can only ever read or write its own data, and the API key is sent only to the chosen AI provider (through the secure relay when the provider requires it). Because data belongs to the account rather than one browser, the electrician can log in from any device and pick up where they left off.

5 - How IBM Bob was used
The platform's structure and functionality were defined before IBM Bob was used to build it. The concept, the architecture, and the way each part of the workflow fits together were planned first, based on a clear vision of what electricians actually need; none of that structure was generated by AI. IBM Bob was then used as a development tool to implement that vision and refine it, not to create it.
The development approach consisted of an iterative back-and-forth: the intended behavior was described, the AI coding assistant proposed and wrote the corresponding code, the result was reviewed, and adjustments were requested until it matched the design. Every product and design decision was made by the human developers; the assistant only executed those decisions.
Reviewing the code was a constant part of this process. The assistant was asked to review, re-check, and verify its own work repeatedly, after nearly every change, rather than having any output trusted on its own (over 30 separate review-and-test cycles across the build). Changes were verified rather than assumed: logic was tested against sample data, interface changes were checked in a real browser, and each file was validated before moving on. This repeated review caught mistakes on several occasions, and they were corrected before reaching the final product.
To support this, realistic sample sets of customer service requests were created and used as test data. These samples, which included vague wording, misspellings, and edge cases similar to real client messages, made it possible to measure how well the system understood requests and to catch problems that would not otherwise have surfaced.
The offline inference engine, which reads and classifies each incoming request, was improved continuously rather than written once and left alone. It went through many rounds of refinement: it was run against the sample requests, the cases it misread were identified, the way it matches language was tightened, and a confidence measure was added so the system can tell when it is unsure. After each round, the updated engine was compared against the previous version to confirm that accuracy genuinely improved without introducing new mistakes.
Finally, AI served a dual role: it was used to help build the platform, and it is also built into the platform as a core feature that understands requests and generates material recommendations. Even so, the product was designed to function without AI as well, through the offline engine, so it remains dependable whether or not an AI provider is connected.
